OVERWATERING — THE #1 KILLER OF HOUSEPLANTS
Overwatering is the most common cause of houseplant death. Signs include yellowing leaves (especially lower leaves), soft or mushy stems, soggy soil that never dries out, mold on the soil surface, and a foul smell from the pot. The roots are essentially suffocating because waterlogged soil prevents oxygen from reaching them. To fix overwatering: stop watering immediately, move the plant to a bright spot with good airflow, check if the pot has drainage holes (add them if not), and consider repotting into fresh dry soil if root rot is suspected. Always check soil moisture before watering by inserting your finger 1-2 inches into the soil.

UNDERWATERING — DROUGHT STRESS
Underwatered plants show wilting or drooping, dry crispy leaf edges, yellowing leaves starting from the tips, bone-dry soil pulling away from pot edges, and lightweight pots. Some plants like peace lilies and fittonias will dramatically droop when thirsty but bounce back quickly after watering. The fix is to water thoroughly until water flows from drainage holes, then allow excess to drain. For severely dehydrated plants, try bottom-watering: place the pot in a tray of water for 30-60 minutes to rehydrate slowly.

ROOT ROT — IDENTIFICATION AND TREATMENT
Root rot is caused by fungi (Pythium, Phytophthora, Fusarium) that thrive in waterlogged conditions. Symptoms visible above soil: yellowing leaves, wilting despite moist soil, dark mushy stems at the base. To diagnose, remove the plant from its pot and inspect roots — healthy roots are white and firm; rotted roots are brown/black, mushy, and may smell foul. Treatment: trim all rotted roots with sterile scissors, dust cuts with cinnamon (natural antifungal) or sulfur, repot in fresh well-draining soil, and water only when the top 2 inches are dry. In severe cases, propagate healthy stem cuttings and discard the original plant.

YELLOW LEAVES — CAUSES AND SOLUTIONS
Yellow leaves can indicate multiple issues. Lower leaves yellowing naturally as they age: normal, no action needed. Widespread yellowing with soft soil: overwatering or root rot. Yellowing with dry soil: underwatering or nutrient deficiency. Pale yellow-green new leaves: nitrogen deficiency, fertilize with balanced liquid fertilizer. Yellow leaves with brown tips: too much fluoride or salt buildup in soil — flush with distilled water. Yellow leaves with green veins: iron or magnesium deficiency (chlorosis) — apply chelated iron or Epsom salt spray. Yellowing on one side only: uneven light distribution — rotate plant.

BROWN LEAF TIPS AND EDGES — CAUSES
Brown tips are almost always caused by low humidity, inconsistent watering, or fluoride/salt toxicity. Low humidity browns: the tips first, especially in winter when heating systems dry the air. Solution: mist regularly, use a pebble tray, or get a humidifier. Underwatering produces brown crispy tips. Fluoride toxicity (common in spider plants, dracaenas) causes brown tips — use filtered or rainwater. Salt buildup from overfertilizing also causes brown tips — flush soil thoroughly with distilled water. Sun scorch causes brown patches or bleaching, not just tips — move plant out of direct sun.

PESTS — COMMON INDOOR PLANT PESTS AND TREATMENTS
Spider Mites: Tiny red or brown mites, appear in dry conditions, produce fine webbing between leaves and stems. Leaves appear stippled, dusty, or bronze. Treatment: rinse with strong water spray, apply neem oil spray or insecticidal soap, increase humidity. Repeat weekly for 3-4 weeks.

Mealybugs: White cottony clusters in leaf axils and undersides, look like white fuzz or rice. Excrete sticky honeydew. Treatment: dab with 70% isopropyl alcohol on cotton swab, apply neem oil, isolate plant immediately to prevent spread.

Fungus Gnats: Small dark flies around soil, larvae damage roots. Caused by consistently moist topsoil. Treatment: allow soil to dry more between waterings, apply sticky yellow traps, drench soil with hydrogen peroxide solution (1:4 ratio), or use Bacillus thuringiensis (Bti) treatment.

Scale: Brown oval bumps firmly attached to stems and leaves, do not move. Excrete sticky residue. Treatment: scrape off manually with toothpick, apply rubbing alcohol with cotton swab, spray with neem oil or horticultural oil. Persistent pest — treat weekly.

Aphids: Soft-bodied green, black, or white insects clustered on new growth and undersides of leaves. Excrete honeydew. Treatment: blast with water, apply insecticidal soap spray, introduce beneficial insects outdoors.

Thrips: Tiny fast-moving insects causing silver streaks and stippling on leaves. Treatment: neem oil, spinosad, or insecticidal soap. Remove heavily damaged leaves.

REPOTTING — WHEN AND HOW
Repot when: roots are circling the bottom, growing out of drainage holes, the plant is top-heavy and tips over easily, soil dries out extremely fast, or the plant has stopped growing despite good care. Best time to repot is spring, when plants enter active growth. Choose a pot only 1-2 inches larger than the current pot — too large a pot causes overwatering problems as excess soil retains moisture. Use fresh potting mix appropriate for the plant type. Water thoroughly after repotting and keep in indirect light for a week to reduce transplant stress.

FERTILIZING — PRINCIPLES AND TIMING
Most houseplants benefit from fertilizing during the growing season (spring and summer), every 2-4 weeks with diluted liquid fertilizer. Reduce to monthly or stop entirely in fall and winter when most plants rest. Signs of over-fertilizing: white crusty buildup on soil surface, brown leaf tips from salt burn, wilting despite adequate water. Solution: flush soil with large amounts of distilled water. Signs of under-fertilizing: pale leaves, slow growth, small new leaves. Use balanced fertilizer (N-P-K like 10-10-10) for foliage plants; high-phosphorus fertilizer for flowering plants.

LIGHT — UNDERSTANDING PLANT LIGHT NEEDS
Low light: north-facing windows, interiors far from windows. Suitable for: snake plants, ZZ plants, pothos, cast iron plants. No plant thrives in zero light — eventually all need some light source.
Medium indirect light: east or west-facing windows several feet back. Suitable for: most tropical foliage plants, peace lilies, Chinese evergreens, philodendrons.
Bright indirect light: bright room but no direct sun hitting leaves. South or west-facing windows with sheer curtains. Suitable for: most tropical plants, monsteras, rubber plants.
Direct sun: south or west windows, unobstructed sunlight. Suitable for: succulents, cacti, citrus, herbs, most edible plants. Direct afternoon sun can scorch tropical plant leaves.

HUMIDITY — WHY IT MATTERS FOR TROPICAL PLANTS
Most tropical houseplants evolved in humid rainforest environments and prefer 40-60% humidity. Indoor humidity, especially in winter with heating systems, often drops to 20-30%, causing brown tips, leaf curl, spider mite infestations, and drooping. Methods to increase humidity: group plants together to create a microclimate, use a pebble tray filled with water (pot sits above water level, not in it), mist plants 2-3 times per week (though this is temporary and can encourage fungal issues), or use a room humidifier (most effective). Avoid misting African violets, succulents, or plants prone to fungal diseases.

SEASONAL CARE ADJUSTMENTS
Spring/Summer: active growth period. Water more frequently, fertilize regularly, repot if needed, watch for increased pest activity with warmth and growth. Move plants closer to light sources.
Fall/Winter: most plants rest or slow down. Reduce watering frequency (soil takes longer to dry), stop or reduce fertilizing, avoid repotting, move plants to brightest available spots as sun angle drops, keep away from cold drafts and heating vents. Watch for spider mite outbreaks in dry heated rooms.

PROPAGATION BASICS
Stem cuttings: cut below a node (where leaf meets stem), remove lower leaves, place in water or moist soil. Most easy houseplants root in 2-4 weeks.
Division: separate root clumps when repotting plants like spider plants, peace lilies, or ZZ plants.
Leaf cuttings: works for succulents, snake plants, African violets. Lay leaf flat on soil or insert upright.
Air layering: for woody plants like rubber trees and fiddle leaf figs that are difficult to root from cuttings.
